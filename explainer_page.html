Bacteria overview

Basic structure of bacterial cells

Bacteria are single-celled prokaryotes that exist virtually everywhere on Earth—in rocks and ice, the ocean's surface, thermal vents, your stomach, and even high in the atmosphere! Like eukaryotes, all bacteria (and archaea) are bounded by a cell membrane, which encloses cytoplasm, DNA, and ribosomes. Unlike eukaryotes, bacteria typically have only 1 chromosome that's shaped in a circle. Most species of bacteria have a cell wall like some eukaryotic cells. However, unlike eukaryotes such as plants, whose cell wall is made of cellulose, bacterium cell walls are made of a complex polysaccharide called peptidoglycan. Bacterial cells and colonies come in many different shapes and sizes. In the first exercise of this activity, you will describe the morphology of colonies and individual cells of different bacterial species. Key term

Peptidoglycan is a rigid mesh-like material in bacterial cell walls. Thickness of this layer is one major reason Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria behave differently during staining.
