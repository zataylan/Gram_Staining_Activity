Bacteria overview

Basic structure of bacterial cells

Bacteria are single-celled prokaryotes found in a huge range of environments. Like other cells, they have a cell membrane, cytoplasm, DNA, and ribosomes. Unlike eukaryotic cells, they do not have a nucleus, and their genetic material is usually organized as a single circular chromosome.

Many bacteria also have a cell wall made of peptidoglycan. That layer matters because it helps determine how bacteria respond during the Gram stain procedure.

Before moving on

Bacterial cells and colonies vary in shape and size. In the next section, you will compare the three common cell shapes used in introductory microbiology: cocci, bacilli, and spirilla.

Key term

Peptidoglycan is a rigid mesh-like material in bacterial cell walls. Thickness of this layer is one major reason Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria behave differently during staining.
