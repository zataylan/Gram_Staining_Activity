Gram stain overview

What the Gram stain tells you

The Gram stain is used to separate bacteria into two broad groups based on cell wall structure. Cells are first stained with crystal violet and iodine, which turns them purple. Alcohol is then used to decolorize the sample. Finally, safranin is added as a counterstain.

The critical difference is what happens after the alcohol step.

Feature Gram-positive bacteria Gram-negative bacteria
Cell wall structure Thick peptidoglycan layer Thin peptidoglycan layer plus an outer membrane
After alcohol step Retains the crystal violet–iodine complex Loses the purple stain
Final appearance Purple Pink or reddish after safranin

Why alcohol matters

Gram-positive cells have a thick peptidoglycan wall. During the alcohol step, that wall tightens enough to trap the purple dye complex inside the cell.

Gram-negative cells have a thinner peptidoglycan layer and an outer membrane rich in lipids. Alcohol disrupts that outer membrane, and the thinner wall cannot hold onto the purple dye. Those cells then take up the safranin counterstain.

Key term

Peptidoglycan is a strong, mesh-like polymer that helps support bacterial cell walls.

Quick knowledge check

Why does alcohol disrupt the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria?

Answer: The outer membrane contains lipid-rich components. Alcohol can disrupt and dissolve those lipids, which damages the membrane and allows the purple stain to wash out.
